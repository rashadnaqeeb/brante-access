Wrath Access Documentation

The Pathfinder Rules

Wrath of the Righteous runs on the Pathfinder First Edition tabletop ruleset (a close cousin of Dungeons & Dragons 3.5). The game handles all the math for you, but understanding the basics makes character building and combat far less opaque. This chapter covers the core ideas; the next applies them to Combat.

Everything here is visible in the game: open your character sheet to see your numbers, and read an ability’s or item’s tooltip to see exactly what it does and why a number is what it is.

The d20 core

Almost every uncertain action resolves the same way: roll a 20-sided die (a d20), add the relevant bonuses, and compare the total to a target number. Meet or beat the target and you succeed.

  • Attacking compares your roll to the enemy’s Armor Class.
  • A skill check or a spell’s resistance compares a roll to a Difficulty Class (DC).
  • Higher is always better. A natural 20 (the die itself showing 20) is special — an automatic hit and a chance at a critical — and a natural 1 automatically fails an attack or save.

Because a single die swings the result by a lot, Pathfinder is about stacking bonuses so the odds are in your favour: buffs, good positioning, the right ability scores, and so on.

Ability scores

Six ability scores describe a character. Each has a modifier — the number actually added to rolls — equal to (score − 10) ÷ 2, rounded down:

  • Strength (STR) — melee attack and damage, carrying capacity, Athletics.
  • Dexterity (DEX) — Armor Class, ranged attacks, Reflex saves, initiative, finesse attacks.
  • Constitution (CON) — hit points and Fortitude saves; nobody dumps it.
  • Intelligence (INT) — wizard spellcasting, extra skill points, knowledge skills.
  • Wisdom (WIS) — cleric/druid spellcasting, Will saves, Perception.
  • Charisma (CHA) — sorcerer/bard/oracle spellcasting, Persuasion, many divine abilities.

Which scores matter most depends entirely on your class.

Classes and levels

Your class is your profession and defines what you can do. Broadly:

  • Martial classes fight with weapons and physical prowess — Fighter, Barbarian, Ranger, Paladin, Slayer, Cavalier, and so on.
  • Casters wield magic — Wizard and Sorcerer (arcane), Cleric and Druid and Oracle (divine), and more. Some prepare a set of spells ahead of time; others cast spontaneously from spells they know.
  • Hybrids mix both — Magus (sword and spell), Inquisitor, Bard, Rogue (skills and precision), and others.

You gain levels by earning experience, up to level 20, choosing new features each time. You can multiclass (take levels in more than one class) and many classes have archetypes — variant versions that swap some features for others. Don’t over-think your first build; the game lets you respec, and an easier difficulty forgives sub-optimal choices.

Hit points

Hit points (HP) are how much damage you can take before falling. You gain HP each level based on your class’s hit die plus your Constitution modifier. Frontline classes have far more than casters, which is why squishy spellcasters stay out of melee.

Armor Class

Armor Class (AC) is how hard you are to hit — the target number an attacker must reach. It starts at 10 and adds your armor, shield, Dexterity, and various magical or situational bonuses. Two variants matter:

  • Touch AC ignores armor and shields (used by spells and effects that only need to touch you), so it’s usually much lower.
  • Flat-footed AC is your AC before you’ve acted, when you lose your Dexterity bonus — being caught flat-footed is dangerous, which is why initiative matters.

Attack bonus

When you attack, you roll a d20 and add your attack bonus: your Base Attack Bonus (BAB) plus the relevant ability modifier (Strength for melee, Dexterity for ranged or finesse) plus other bonuses. BAB rises with level, faster for martial classes. At high BAB you gain iterative attacks — extra attacks per full attack at +6, +11, and +16 BAB — so a high-level fighter swings several times in a round while a wizard does not.

Saving throws

When something happens to you — a fireball, a poison, a charm — you make a saving throw to resist or reduce it. There are three:

  • Fortitude (Fort) — Constitution-based; resists poison, disease, and raw physical effects.
  • Reflex (Ref) — Dexterity-based; dodges area effects like explosions.
  • Will — Wisdom-based; resists mind-affecting magic, fear, and illusions.

Each class has good or poor progression in each save, so most characters have an obvious weak save an enemy can exploit — and that you can shore up with items and buffs.

Skills

Skills cover non-combat (and some combat) competence. You invest ranks as you level, and a check is the d20 plus ranks plus the governing ability modifier (plus a bonus if it’s a “class skill” for you). Wrath of the Righteous uses a streamlined skill list:

  • Athletics (STR) — climbing, swimming, breaking free, some combat maneuvers.
  • Mobility (DEX) — avoiding attacks of opportunity, acrobatic movement.
  • Trickery (DEX) — disabling traps, picking locks, sleight of hand.
  • Stealth (DEX) — moving unseen.
  • Knowledge (Arcana) and Knowledge (World) (INT) — identifying magic, monsters, and lore.
  • Lore (Nature) and Lore (Religion) (WIS) — natural and divine knowledge, identifying creatures.
  • Perception (WIS) — spotting hidden things, traps, and ambushes.
  • Persuasion (CHA) — the main dialogue skill (diplomacy, bluff, intimidate rolled into one).
  • Use Magic Device (CHA) — using scrolls and wands beyond your own class.

A balanced party covers the important ones; Perception, Trickery, and Persuasion come up constantly.

Feats

Feats are discrete special abilities you pick at certain levels — extra attacks, bonuses to a fighting style, metamagic that enhances spells, and so on. They’re a big part of how you specialize a character. The game shows each feat’s effect and any prerequisites when you select it.

Conditions, buffs, and debuffs

During play, characters pick up temporary conditions. Positive ones (buffs) come from spells and abilities — blessed, hasted, blurred, enlarged. Negative ones (debuffs) come from enemies — fatigued, frightened, sickened, prone, paralyzed, and many more. Much of Pathfinder’s tactical depth is in applying debuffs to enemies while protecting your own party from them. You can review every condition currently affecting a character at any time.

Alignment

Characters have an alignment on two axes — lawful to chaotic, and good to evil. Your choices nudge it, and it gates a little content (some classes and mythic paths care about it). It’s flavour and consequence more than a stat you optimize.

Where to see all of this

You don’t have to memorize any of it. Your character sheet lays out every number with a breakdown, tooltips explain where a bonus comes from, and the in-game encyclopedia holds the rules reference. When a number surprises you, read its tooltip — it almost always explains itself.

# How the Game Works

If you're new to this style of game, this chapter explains the big picture before the next two dig
into the rules and combat. None of it is mod-specific — it's how Pathfinder: Wrath of the Righteous
works for everyone. The chapters that follow are [The Pathfinder Rules](rules.md) and
[Combat](combat.md).

## What a CRPG is

Pathfinder: Wrath of the Righteous is a **CRPG** — a computer role-playing game in the tradition of
Baldur's Gate, Pillars of Eternity, and the earlier Pathfinder: Kingmaker. You don't control a
single hero in real time the way you might in an action game. Instead you direct a **party** of
characters from an overhead view, issuing commands: go here, attack that, cast this, open that chest,
talk to this person.

The game loop is: **explore** an area, **talk** to people and make choices, **fight** when fights
happen, **loot** and manage your gear, **rest** to recover, and **level up** as you earn experience —
all in service of a branching story you shape through your decisions. It's a long game built from
many small tactical and roleplaying choices rather than reflexes.

## Your character and your party

You begin by **creating your main character** (the story calls them the Knight Commander). You'll
choose a class, ability scores, skills, and so on — the next chapter explains what those mean. This
character carries the story and can't be removed from the party.

As you play you **recruit companions** — fully-written characters who join you. Your **active party
holds up to six** characters, including your main character. You can have more companions than that
and swap who's in the party at camp. Importantly, **animal companions, mounts, and summoned
creatures don't count toward the six** — a druid's bear or a summoned monster is extra, which makes
those builds powerful.

Your party shares **one inventory** and one pool of money. You don't shuffle items between characters
to carry them; anything one character picks up is available to all. You do still **equip** each
character individually (their weapons, armor, rings, and so on).

## Exploring

You move your party around hand-crafted areas. You direct them to a spot and they walk there,
**pathfinding automatically** around obstacles — you point, they figure out the route. Along the way
you find people to talk to, containers and bodies to loot, traps and locks, and triggers that advance
the story.

The game has **fog of war** and line of sight: you only know what your characters can currently
perceive. Move closer to reveal what's ahead. Many areas also hide things behind **Perception**
checks (spotting a hidden door or a trap) and gate locks and traps behind **Trickery**.

## Real time and pausing

By default the game runs in **real time**: your party and every enemy act continuously, without
waiting for you to decide. You're directing a live scene, not taking neat turns.

The tool that makes this manageable is the **pause**. You can pause at any moment (press Space) — mid
exploration or mid fight — to take stock, check your characters, and issue orders with no time
pressure, then unpause to let everyone carry them out. This "real-time with pause" rhythm is how the
game is meant to be played: **pause often**, give commands, unpause to watch them resolve, pause
again. Trying to play it as a reflex game in real time will not go well.

The game can also **pause itself** on important events — combat beginning, a new enemy appearing, a
character dropping low, a spell finishing — so it stops for you at the moments that matter. Which of
these trigger an auto-pause is configurable in the game's options.

If you'd rather not manage a live clock at all, you can switch the whole game to **turn-based** mode,
where everyone acts one at a time in order. Either way, how you actually command your characters is
covered in [Combat](combat.md).

## Talking and making choices

Conversations are central. Dialogue often offers **skill-check options** — a line you can only pick,
or that succeeds, if your character is good enough at Persuasion, Lore, and so on. Choices can shift
your **alignment** (where you sit on the lawful–chaotic and good–evil axes), change how characters
react to you, open or close quests, and steer the larger story. There's rarely one "right" answer;
the game is built around consequences.

## Resting

Adventuring drains resources — hit points, spells, and abilities that recharge "per day." You restore
them by **resting** (making camp). Resting advances time and lets you assign camp roles — who cooks
(a good meal can grant a buff) and who keeps watch against an ambush, among others. You can't rest
just anywhere, and resting in dangerous places risks being attacked, so it's a deliberate decision,
not a free heal button.

## Getting stronger

Characters earn **experience** and gain **levels** (up to 20 in their classes). Each level you make
build choices — new class features, feats, skill ranks, and for spellcasters new spells. Wrath of the
Righteous also layers on its signature system, **mythic paths**: partway into the story your character
gains mythic power and chooses a path — Angel, Demon, Lich, Trickster, Azata, Aeon, and more — that
grants its own escalating abilities on top of your normal class. You don't need to understand mythic
paths to start; the game introduces them in time.

## Difficulty

The game's difficulty is **highly adjustable**. You can pick a preset or tune individual sliders —
enemy strength, how lethal combat is, whether you take a story-focused or a hardcore tactical
experience, and much more. You can change these at almost any time, so if combat feels too punishing
(or too easy), adjust it rather than grind. New players are well served by an easier preset and
**turn-based** combat while learning the rules.

## The crusade

Beyond the personal adventure, Wrath of the Righteous has a second, optional-feeling layer: a
**crusade** against the demonic invasion, with army management and a strategic map. It runs alongside
the main story and has its own systems. It's secondary to the core adventuring and combat this primer
focuses on; you can engage with it lightly or deeply depending on your settings.

## Saving

Save often and in multiple slots — CRPGs reward experimentation, and an autosave alone isn't enough
when a single dialogue choice or a hard fight can send the story in a new direction.
